This isn't an article about productivity. It's about a tension I can't resolve. It's about a set of questions I keep thinking about over and over, without any specific path forward. 

- *How do we stay relevant in a world where AI is better at doing most of the tasks than the a skilled human being?*
- *Is this different than any other big technological breakthrough? Does it feel different only because we're living in it?*
- *How do we build knowledge and skills when spinning the wheel becomes addictive?*

I hope writing this out will help me clarify my ideas.

## The surface-level stuff doesn't matter

Every few weeks there's a new tool, a new interface, a new way of working, a new benchmark to be crushed. 

And as the time passes and more vc-funded businesses generate indisputable claims on the new Sacred Graal of productivity and prepare to lay-off another 10% of the staff, people like me are chasing the latest update and testing it out on some supposedly improved internal workflow.

It's a never ending wheel. Is it progress?

And maybe more importantly, how do we make progress in such a situation?

People obsess about the latest app or interface: going from browser apps, to CLI workflows, back to the app. A UI-based waltz where we risk losing the point on what really matters: building transferable skills that are independent from the tool.

We're at the very start of the adoption curve, and one way to escape the circle of pointless FOMO is to remember why we do work: to achieve goals and to learn new stuff.

And if learning is such a central "why" in our life, then it's important to remember that what actually transfers across UIs and models is not the ability to build a SKILL.md with the appropriate references (at the time of writing, this is considered an advanced aspect of Claude Code based workflows. In a couple of months it probably won't be), but it is the thinking that produced those configurations. Knowing what a good activation flow looks like. Evaluating which are the right metrics to track based on different changes. Recognizing when an API design is going to cause problems downstream. Understanding why one database schema will scale and another won't. 

That knowledge moves with you regardless of what tool you're typing into. Or talking to. 

But when the entire industry relies on hype to keep raising gigantic sums of capital, it can be very easy to lose the compass and get derailed. Shiny object syndrome at its peak in the corporate world.

## The ebook problem

Remember when every SaaS company had a free ebook? Download "The Complete Guide to X" and suddenly you're an expert. Except you weren't, because reading about something and doing it are different activities that build different things in your brain.

Skill files are the new ebooks. You can download someone's carefully crafted workflow, but without the reps that built it, you don't know when it's failing you. You don't know what it's optimizing for. You can't tell when the context has shifted enough that the skill is now actively harmful.

You need the scar tissue that comes from doing the work badly, then less badly, then well. There's no shortcut for that. Or at least, no one has found one yet.

## The averaging machine

AI models are, at their core, averaging machines. They're trained on enormous amounts of human output and they produce things that look like a sophisticated blend of that output. Better context leads to better results. That's real. But there's a ceiling.

If everyone uses AI for execution, and AI pulls toward the mean, then everyone's output starts to converge. The baseline rises, which is genuinely good. But the question becomes: how do you stand out when the floor is "pretty good"?

The answer people keep landing on is taste. Creative judgment. The instinct for what to ship and what to kill. Knowing that something technically works but isn't right.

That sounds correct to me. But it opens a harder question.

## Where do the reps come from?

Taste isn't a personality trait. It's built through repetition. You develop judgment by making things, evaluating them honestly, failing, adjusting, and doing it again. Thousands of times across years.

If AI handles the execution, the actual making, where do those reps come from?

Maybe they shift. Maybe instead of reps in "how to build an onboarding flow," you get reps in "how to evaluate whether this onboarding flow works." Craft shifts from creation to curation and judgment.

That's possible. I'm genuinely not sure if it's true or if it's a convenient story we tell ourselves to feel better about delegating more and more.

## The loop I can't stop thinking about

This is the part that keeps me up:

Tools get better. So you delegate more. Fewer reps. Weaker judgment. You can't tell when output is mediocre. So you accept it. You fall further behind people who can tell. Repeat.

The cruel part is that it feels productive the whole time. You're shipping. You're moving fast. Dylan Martin called it "the same dopamine loop as pulling a slot machine lever." Low effort, variable reward, endlessly repeatable. Speed and fun are the bribe for surrendering expertise.

You don't notice the drift until you're competing against someone with actual taste and you can't figure out why their work resonates and yours doesn't.

I don't think this is hypothetical. I think it's already happening.

## The choice

There's a new kind of gap forming, and it's not about access to tools. Everyone will have access. The gap is between people who develop the judgment to use these tools meaningfully and people who let the tools do their thinking for them.

Early adopters have a head start. Not because they learned the tools first, but because they've been doing the reps. Experimenting, evaluating, building the instinct for what "good" looks like in a world where "decent" is free.

Most people will pick cheap execution. It's the rational short-term move. The output is good enough. The speed is intoxicating. And the cost, a slow erosion of judgment, is invisible until it isn't.

I'm not sure there's a way out of this. I'm not sure there needs to be. Maybe the world just changes and "taste" becomes as niche a concern as calligraphy became after the printing press. Maybe that's fine.

But I don't think so. And I think naming the choice clearly is worth something, even without a solution attached.

Cheap execution now, or durable judgment later. That's the trade. Make it with your eyes open.
